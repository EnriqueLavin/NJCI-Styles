The checklist you need before diving into a cannabis business

Starting up a business in the cannabis space, like any business, requires a few essential elements — a business plan, time, capital, inspiration and sweat equity, along with the ability to avoid potential missteps.

“Often we get calls from folks interested in starting up a cannabis dispensary or cannabis retail store,” said Scott Rudder, president of the New Jersey CannaBusiness Association. “My common response is whatever it will take for you to pursue a liquor store license and shop, and then some, is what it will take to start a cannabis business.”

Cannabis industry insiders agree there are several necessities when starting up a cannabusiness:

  • Financing. Lots of it, at least $1 million to $2 million to show you have the bankroll to support a successful operation.
  • Experience running a successful business. And if you’re looking to start up a cultivation operation, you’ll likely need to demonstrate you have experience growing cannabis professionally (not in your mom’s basement).
  • A history of paying taxes.
  • The ability to pass an intensive — almost proctological — level of scrutiny in a review of your personal and professional background. Additionally, all of your business partners and associates will likely need to pass the same level of vetting.
  • A New Jersey residence.
  • A company name that does not include the words weed, marijuana or 420.

Vikas Desai of WelCan Capital, a private investment group focused on the cannabis industry, said it’s “crucial for those in the industry to act with good partners due to stringent regulations and requirements.”

“One failed background check could be the difference between obtaining a license and not,” he said.

Moreover, according to Rudder, if you’re not already taking the steps necessary to get your application ready, then you’re already behind.

“The people who are most serious about this industry are already taking the steps necessary,” he said. “If you’re waiting until legalization to start your business, you won’t be able to compete. You need to start taking active steps today.”

‘Just stay calm’ and other advice from employment attorneys to cannabis employers

As New Jersey inches closer to legalization, many employers have been left wondering where the current state of the law leaves them with respect to their rights and potential exposure when it comes to employee cannabis use.

A January panel at the New Jersey Business & Industry Association’s called “The Cannabis Economy: Are You Ready?” featured attorneys providing insight to employers and business operators on cannabis policy in the workplace.

Connell Foley partner Leo Hurley presents at @NJBIA's "Cannabis Economy: Are You Ready" seminar. #njcannabis #cannabislaw #cannabisindustry pic.twitter.com/YftQzrZM7e

— Connell Foley LLP (@ConnellFoleyLLP) January 23, 2019

“The advice that I’ve been giving employers is ‘just stay calm,’” Archer Law attorney Laura Link said.

Archer Law attorney Laura Link

While the current state medical marijuana law allows individuals who’ve received a valid recommendation from a physician to purchase, possess and consume cannabis, the statute leaves patients out in the cold with respect to employment.

Employers are not required to accommodate them under the law. Moreover, employees who consume cannabis at work are fired regardless of whether they have a valid card.

One of the biggest challenges is the lack of precision with testing as it creates situations in which employees test positive for cannabis at work but their use may have occurred outside of the workplace and on their own time.

As Connell Foley attorney Leo Hurley noted, the recent federal decision in Cotto v. Ardagh Glass Packing provides a significant amount of guidance to employers regarding the current status of the law. In that case, the judge held employers are not required to waive mandatory drug testing for any employee — even if they’re a medical marijuana patient.

Connell Foley attorney Leo Hurley

The federal courts may be currently siding with employers on decisions such as this, but, Hurley said, that’ll likely change.

“I tend to believe the courts in New Jersey are going to be a bit more permissive as a general rule on this issue, but that doesn't mean there's not going to be a fight,” he said. “I think it's something that is going to wind up being litigated to the Supreme Court.”

Even as state law changes, employees whose jobs are subject to federal law and regulation are barred from using cannabis in or out of work — regardless of their status with the New Jersey medical marijuana program.

Employers are still able to drug test new hires, but Link advised the best course of action is not to ask whether or not they have a medical card — as failure to secure employment could open the employer up to a potential medical discrimination claim.

“Once you ask that question you can't close that box back up,” she said.

Blanket drug testing after workplace accidents remains off the table for employers due to a 2016 OSHA decision, Link said. The new rule permits drug testing of employees if there’s suspicion that impairment led to the accident, in which case, Link said, the best course of action is document that suspicion.

Not many easy answers when it comes to solving the financing puzzle

Banks are reticent to comply with the numerous guidelines regulating leaf-touching businesses (typically cultivation, extraction, transportation and retail) and file regular suspicious activity reports (SARs) with the federal government, insiders say. As a result, banks veer heavily on the side of caution by eschewing lending, resulting in a lot of cash being tucked under a lot of mattresses.

Without banks, only a couple options remain — typically credit unions or private investors.

“It’s hard to get traditional sources of financing,” said Vikas Desai of WelCan Capital, which invests in cannabis businesses. “As long as the federal status is classified as a controlled substance, those markets are not going to be available.”  

Moreover, those few banks willing to do business with operators in the cannabis industry come at a hefty price.

Mike Nahass, president of Terra Tech, a vertically-integrated agricultural company with cannabis operations on the West Coast, said his company pays a monthly fee of $1,500 per account to have access to banking services. The upside is that, unlike other dispensaries, customers of Terra Tech’s retail brands can use their debit and credit cards instead of having to pay in cash.

Banking is also available directly through the Federal Reserve, according to Rick Frimmer, the head of EisnerAmper’s national cannabis group. A state bank in New Jersey, as Gov. Phil Murphy has proposed, is also a possibility, Frimmer said, but that institution wouldn’t be insured by the FDIC and would require state backing to insure its accounts.

Larry Lipman, CEO of Alliance Financial Network, however, doesn’t think a state bank is likely because of the amount of money and time needed to create the bank — Lipman estimates about $2 billion and two years — and the strength of the banking lobby. Additionally, he said, political change in the wind in the form of mid-term elections could have banks changing their tune after November.

Gov. Phil Murphy announced another boost to the medicinal marijuana program last week when he declared opioid addiction would be added to the list of qualifying conditions, effective immediately.

Once again, the Democratic governor demonstrated his commitment to expand the program, which had been stifled by the prior administration for eight years.

N.J. Gov. Phil Murphy.

The Empire State

Take Pennsylvania, where the program is only 11 months old with 45 dispensaries and 12 grower/processors. There are 76,000 certified patients, with another 29,000 who are either awaiting approval or who haven’t made a purchase yet, state Health Department spokeswoman Nate Hardle said. There are also 945 participating doctors.

In New York, 87,855 patients have been recommended by 2,154 doctors in the four years since the program has been active, according to the state’s website. Th Empire State has 30 dispensaries and cultivators

With such a head start and a smaller population than its neighbors, (12.8 million people in Pennsylvania, 19.8 million in New York), local advocates stress that New Jersey, with 9 million people, should have had a far larger advantage. It has has six active dispensaries/cultivators with another six on the way this year.

Cory Booker's marijuana social justice bill is gaining momentum

Note: This article originally was published on Jan. 31, 2019.

As the new Congress begins to debate cannabis legislation, don’t be surprised if U.S. Sen. Cory Booker’s Marijuana Justice Act gets some traction.

The bill, which the New Jersey Democrat introduced in the summer of 2017, would end the federal ban on marijuana, leaving it up to the states to decide what they want to do.

Passage of the bipartisan criminal justice act, which offers alternatives to incarceration and shorter prison terms to those convicted of nonviolent drug crimes, may help pave the way for Booker’s bill.

U.S. Sen. Cory Booker, D-N.J.

The legislation sought to reverse the lock-them-up mentality of the 1990s, when nonviolent drug offenders — disproportionally members of minority groups — were put behind bars, sometimes for life.

“There is an increased understanding of the negative impact that the war on drugs has had,” said Morgan Fox, a spokesman for the National Cannabis Industry Association. “There’s a lot more comfortableness with addressing that issue now.”

While leaving the decision on legalizing marijuana to the states, Booker’s bill also would provide federal incentives to states to change their cannabis laws if they were shown to disproportionately hurt low-income individuals or people of color.

In addition, people already convicted of marijuana-related crimes could have their sentences reviewed by a judge.

Combine the bipartisan support for criminal justice legislation with the fact that more and more states are legalizing marijuana, either for medical or personal use. That has resulted in a larger number of lawmakers in both parties who want the federal government to drop its ban on the drug.

“I’m really optimistic with the changing landscape. In the large sort of panorama of criminal justice reform, this is something we can move on,” Booker told NJ Cannabis Insider.

It was a Republican, U.S. Sen. Cory Gardner of Colorado, who sought unsuccessfully to amend the criminal justice bill to include a provision leaving it up to the states rather than the federal government to decide whether to legalize or ban cannabis.

Gardner is one of the sponsors of the bipartisan Strengthening the Tenth Amendment Through Entrusting States, or STATES Act.

Fox said Booker’s bill goes even further.

“It does a lot of things regarding social justice that the STATES Act doesn’t necessarily do,” Fox said. “This is an issue of primary importance when discussing legalization.”

Justin Strekal, political director for NORML, the National Organization for the Reform of Marijuana Laws, said the Booker bill may have better prospects in the House.

The House version of Booker’s bill garnered 44 sponsors in the last Congress, 10 percent of the chamber. Strekal called it “a historic level of support.”

6 tips for starting a cannabis business in N.J., as told by a successful Colorado cannabis CEO

By Andy Williams,
CEO of Medicine Man and co-founder of Medicine Man Technologies

Andy Williams

Gov. Phil Murphy unveiled plans last month to expand New Jersey’s medical marijuana program, and coupled with his election promise to legalize adult-use marijuana, many entrepreneurs are anticipating opportunities for launching their own cannabis enterprise.

If you’re one of these entrepreneurs, you may be wondering where to start. As someone with nearly 10 years of experience in Colorado’s cannabis industry, here are a couple hard-learned tips on how to prepare for the potential arrival of this industry in the Garden State.

Follow the legislation. As of now -- despite Murphy’s support -- regulated recreational marijuana is still theoretical. You can follow the status of various bills put forward to legalize sales of adult-use marijuana on the New Jersey Legislature’s website. This is also where the text of bills regarding regulation may show up, although many states create a regulatory committee or task force to hash out details. You can also follow NJ Cannabis Insider’s vote tracker, which periodically polls state lawmakers about where they stand on legalization.

Be proactive. Lobby your legislators regarding their votes on legalization legislation. Join industry organizations like NJCBA and attend national cannabis conferences. Most states have public hearings regarding legalization and regulations. Get your name out there as someone who cares about the future of the industry, and work to get involved in the rulemaking process.

Be patient. Green rush fever is already sweeping New Jersey, as people race to stake claims on industrial properties, greenhouses and so on. But there are so many variables and unknowns, I don’t recommend doing any of that until we see whether legislation passes and what the rules are. Once legislation allowing cannabis sales is passed, it takes most states at least eight to 12 months to establish initial regulations. In order to understand what you’ll need to do to qualify to open a business, you’ll have to see what parameters are established. Additionally, cities and counties almost always have a say about cannabis in their jurisdiction, including whether and where cultivation facilities and dispensaries can operate. You might consider working with an experienced cannabis consultant, like my own Medicine Man Technologies or another group, to help you with these responsibilities.

Be prepared. You’re going to need to meet the requirements of your state to work in the industry. In Colorado, that means applying for a license, but the process and requirements vary by state, so you’ll need to check your government websites once rules are put into place or changed. For New Jersey, that’s currently the Department of Health, which oversees the medical marijuana program.

Do the extra-credit homework. Just because the rules aren’t yet written, that doesn’t mean you can’t do research now. Spend a few days in states like Colorado, Oregon or Washington where adult-use sales have been happening for years. Visit dispensaries and take notes on the customer experience. Talk to the owners, if you can, and other people who work there. Take time to educate yourself about how the industry has developed in other states. You can learn from their successes and failures. Also take a look through the rules established by a state such as Colorado or Oregon; it’s important to know what is involved and be aware that it’s an evolving process.

Be careful whom you consult for advice. Seek competent, recommended advisers. Be wary of people who want you to pay before they talk to you. Most reputable consultants are happy to arrange an initial phone or in-person meeting free of charge. Also be wary of people in the consulting business who are not currently involved in cultivation. Even if they operated a facility 10 years ago, so much has changed in recent years that most of their advice will be outdated. Look for people who practice what they preach.

Exciting times are ahead for New Jersey — and plenty of uncertainty. Getting educated on what’s involved with a state opening the door for adult-use cannabis sales should help entrepreneurs evaluate whether it’s the right move for them.
